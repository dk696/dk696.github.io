Research

My scientific interest is to improve our understanding of processes within the interior of the Earth that involve interactions between materials, from groundwater aquifers and crustal magmatic systems at shallow depths, subducting slabs at intermediate depths, to ultra-low velocity zones in the Core-Mantle Boundary region. As a seismologist, this entails using seismic waves to map and track interactions between materials that form our planet. My efforts as an Earth scientist therefore focus on developing and implementing new ways of extracting information from geophysical data (via machine learning) to develop quantitative multi-scale models, initially focusing on aquifer systems and plume generation at the core mantle boundary. As a collaborator on the InSight Mission to Mars, I am also actively involved in planetary research, focusing on characterizing the interior structure of the Mars by leveraging techniques developed for studying Earth structures.

Ultra-low velocity zones of the Pacific basin

Understanding the core-mantle boundary region is critical for constraining the origin of plumes, fate of subducted slabs, and nature of primate geochemical reservoirs. One of the greatest mysteries of the region is the nature of seismically anomalous regions including continental-scale large low shear velocity provinces (LLSVPs) beneath the Pacific and Africa, and much smaller patches called Ultra-Low Velocity Zones (ULVZs) that are characterized by dramatically reduced velocities (e.g., ~30% and 10% for Vp and Vs, respectively). Recently, LLSVPs and ULVZs have been tied to isotopic heterogeneities measured in ocean island basalts. With collaborators at the University of Maryland and Johns Hopkins University, I systematically analyze scattering across the Pacific basin by using a new technique called "sequencing" developed by Prof. Brice Menard at Johns Hopkins and Dalya Baron at Tel Aviv University to contextualize thousands of seismograms of waves diffracting along the Core-Mantle boundary (Kim et al., 2020, Science). This allowed me to detect pervasive heterogeneity in the Pacific region that includes the strongest signal due to a plume root beneath Hawaii and a previously unrecognized ULVZ beneath the Marquesas Islands.

Using seismic observation for studying groundwater hydrology

Over 2 billion people worldwide suffer from water stress. Even though groundwater in large aquifer systems is often relied upon as a water source, it is one of the least well‐known components of the hydrologic cycle. Geodetic and gravity measurements often provide useful insights on groundwater changes but they suffer from limited spatial and temporal resolution. In my recent work with Prof. Ved Lekic, we suggest an alternative and/or complementary approach for monitoring groundwater changes using seismic data (Kim et al., 2019, GRL). Specifically, both ambient noise autocorrelation and teleseismic receiver functions computed at a single seismic station show consistent correlations with groundwater level (GWL) changes on long timescales in two large aquifers of the U.S. (the Gulf Coast Aquifer System and the Florida Aquifer System). We find that an abrupt groundwater level rise of ~15m following three consecutive 2008 hurricanes, resulted in a 1.2% increase in Vp determined from autocorrelations and caused variations in power spectra density of receiver functions. Our simple physical model suggests that GWL changes affect Vp more strongly than Vs, consistent with our observations and those of other researchers using inter-station correlations. The current challenge is to quantitatively tie seismic constraints on the depth-distribution of groundwater to key hydraulic parameters, ultimately allowing data collected by seismic arrays to be used to map changes of those parameters over time.

Exploring fine details of geologic features at depth

In my Ph.D. dissertation, I focused on bridging the gap between high-resolution seismic imaging based on industry-style reflection surveys and large-scale imaging based on earthquake data. I developed techniques that enable the resolution of very fine details of subsurface structure across different scales but using broadband seismic stations and earthquake data. Specifically, I used scattered/converted body waves from various seismic sources (vibrations from earthquakes; Kim et al., 2017; 2018; 2019, train noise; Quiros et al., 2016, and active sources; Kim and Brown, 2019) to image the subsurface from upper crustal structures (geothermal/magmatic reservoirs) to subduction zones. Through these studies, I explored very fine details of geological features at depth and attempted to bridge the gap toward features at a scale one would obtain from reflection seismology but using passive seismic sources (Kim et al, 2019). For example, I was able to image a series of sill-like crustal magma bodies beneath Krafla geothermal system in Iceland including a very small rhyolitic magma pocket at a depth of ~2km, unexpectedly encountered by a borehole. Together with waveform simulations, I could draw a convincing interpretation of a distributed magmatic system at Krafla rather than a large pervasively molten magma chamber. Moreover, these approaches allowed me to characterize the subducting slab interface in central Alaskan megathrust at greater depths. My work suggests a lithologic low-velocity channel overriding the slab, which is effectively invisible to traditional teleseismic receiver function imaging.

Obtaining robust seismic constraints from planetary explorations

I am actively involved in planetary research, with the goal of characterizing the interior structure of the Mars by leveraging techniques developed for studying Earth structures. As a collaborator on the InSight Mission to Mars, I use marsquake recordings collected by the SEIS (Seismic Explorations for Interior Structure) instrument to explore the crustal thickness and layering of Mars (Knapmeyer-Endrun, et al., in prep.). I have also used the autocorrelations of scattered marsquake waves to identify signatures of waves bouncing from intra-crustal discontinuities (Compaire, et el., in review). As interest in resource extraction and manned exploration of small planetary bodies grows, planetary analog studies are becoming a crucial tool for assessing and refining geophysical exploration methods. At the University of Maryland, I am an active member of the GEODES (The Geophysical Exploration of the Dynamics and Evolution of the Solar Systems) Virtual Institute, working with Profs. Ved Lekic and Nick Schmerr to develop geophysical strategies for upcoming lunar and asteroid science missions. Specifically, my research is to develop and evaluate an active seismic imaging approach that can maximize the utility of seismic waveform data while quantifying uncertainties of inferred subsurface properties. Importantly, our method allows straightforward extension to joint inversion with other available geophysical measurements (e.g., gravity, GPR, magnetics). The successful implementation of this method and its validation in a planetary analog environment will inform future geophysical exploration of the SSERVI target bodies. See NESF2020 abstract for details.
